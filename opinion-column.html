Opinion

I Was Supposed to Be the Best. Now, I’m Not.

The views expressed in this column are the author's personal reflections as a student.

Once upon a time, I was someone who seemed to be ahead of everyone my age. I could read faster, write better, and speak more clearly. I understood things quickly, sometimes even faster than the people around me. I used to finish tasks before time was up and understand lessons before others even finished reading them.

But now… I’m not that person anymore.

Before, people around me pressured me because I was known as the “advanced” or “intelligent” one. A lot of my value seemed to come from academic achievements. Now the pressure does not really come from them anymore. Instead, it comes from me.

Somehow the expectations that others once had for me became my own expectations. I keep comparing myself to who I used to be and who I thought I would become. Because of that, even small failures feel very big to me.

Sometimes I am afraid of realizing that I am no longer the intelligent child I used to be.

“Now I feel like I’m falling behind in the same place that once felt easy.”

This time last year, sitting in class felt completely different. I felt confident and capable. The lessons were easier to understand. Now those same lessons feel harder, almost like a language I can hear but cannot fully understand.

I see people around me raising their hands and answering questions while I hesitate. They seem to understand things faster than I do now, and sometimes I cannot even finish what I start.

It makes me feel like the person I used to be disappeared.

Everywhere I go, I feel like I carry the memory of that old version of myself. It follows me into exams, assignments, and every blank page I struggle to answer.

Sometimes I wonder if that version of me even really existed, or if it was simply something people believed about me that I also began to believe.

Maybe the child who seemed to know everything didn’t really know herself.

Maybe I was just moving faster than everyone else for a while.

Maybe I never learned how to stay there.

And maybe there are others like me — people who were once called “the smart one,” now quietly trying to figure out who they are without that title.

Artificial intelligence has become a silent study partner for many students.

Opinion

When Prompting Replaced Thinking

The views expressed in this column reflect the author's thoughts about AI and student learning.

I’ll admit it: I use ChatGPT. For a while, I used it a lot.

At first, it felt like discovering a secret academic advantage. Whenever I needed help explaining a concept, starting an essay late at night, or generating ideas for a project, the solution was simple — open a chat box and ask.

Within seconds, artificial intelligence could summarize readings, clarify complicated topics, and organize thoughts faster than most textbooks.

For students overwhelmed by deadlines and academic pressure, that kind of assistance feels almost miraculous.

But somewhere along the way, I noticed something uncomfortable.

I realized I was no longer simply using AI as a tool. I had started depending on it.

“At some point, my first instinct stopped being to think. My first instinct became prompting.”

The realization came during an ordinary assignment. I caught myself wondering what I would even write if I didn’t open ChatGPT first.

That question stayed with me longer than I expected. It forced me to ask something strangely personal for a school task: who would I be academically without AI?

Students today do not just use artificial intelligence — we have developed a culture around identifying it.

People read a paragraph and immediately say, “That sounds like AI.” Sometimes the evidence is almost humorous. A single punctuation mark like an em dash suddenly becomes proof that a machine wrote the sentence.

Yet there is something ironic about this trend. While students have become skilled at detecting AI writing, many of us spend less time improving our own writing.

Tools like ChatGPT can still be genuinely helpful. They can explain difficult concepts and help generate ideas.

The problem begins when helpful tools become intellectual shortcuts.

When we rely on AI for every explanation or paragraph, something subtle begins to disappear: the struggle of thinking.

And that struggle is not a flaw in learning. It is the learning process itself.

Writing is messy. Thinking is difficult. But it is through revision, confusion, and persistence that understanding grows.

Artificial intelligence removes much of that struggle — and sometimes with it, our confidence in our own ability to think independently.

I still use ChatGPT sometimes. It remains a useful tool.

But the ideas that truly matter — our perspectives, opinions, and voices — must still come from somewhere else.

Ideally, they come from us.
